Recommended Reading

Fosang AJ and Colbran RJ (2015) Transparency is the key to quality. J Biol Chem. 290: 29692-94.

This JBC editorial describes the “three major gaps” identified in overall data reporting. Gaps include “the need for (i) more complete disclosure of experimental design and reporting of experimental uncertainty and reproducibility, (ii) improved statistical and graphical presentation of quantitative data, and (iii) revised guidelines for the presentation and quantitation of immuno (“Western”) blots."

Janes KA (2015) An analysis of critical factors for quantitative immunoblotting. Sci Signal 8(371): rs2.

This detailed study examines the role of sample preparation, protocol implementation, detection scheme, and normalization approach in quantitative Western blot analysis. It describes diagnostic experiments for evaluation of accuracy and precision in the immunoblot analysis workflow, and shows how “pseudoquantitative” immunoblot data can over- or underestimate protein abundance.

McDonough AA, Veiras LC, Minas JN, and Ralph DL (2015) Considerations when quantitating protein abundance by immunoblot. Am J Physiol Cell Physiol 308: C426-33.

This review outlines important issues in quantitative analysis of protein abundance. It describes how attention to sample integrity, antibody specificity, linearity of the detection system, and acceptable loading controls can improve quantitative immunoblotting.

Aldridge GM, Podrebarac DM, Greenough WT and Waller IJ (2008) The use of total protein stains as loading controls: an alternative to high-abundance single protein controls in semi-quantitative immunoblotting. J Neurosci Meth. 172(2): 250-54.

This study examines the role of the internal loading control in Western blot normalization, and evaluates the impact of overloading on loading control performance. It reports that abundant controls such as GAPDH and β-actin do not accurately reflect differences in protein concentration, emphasizing the need for validation of each internal loading control. Total protein staining is recommended as an alternative.

Eaton SL, Roche SL, Llavero Hurtado M, Oldknow KJ, Farquharson C, Gillingwater TH, and Wishart TM (2013) Total protein analysis as a reliable loading control for quantitative fluorescent Western blotting. PLoS ONE 8(8): e72457.

This study demonstrates differential expression of commonly used internal reference proteins (actin and tubulin), not only between tissue types but in different portions of the same tissue. Total protein analysis is explored as a more robust approach to Western blot normalization.

Degasperi A, Birtwistle MR, Volinsky N, Rauch J, Kolch W, Kholodenko BN (2014) Evaluating strategies to normalise biological replicates of Western blot data. PLoS ONE 9(1): e87293.

This analysis explores the effects of Western blot normalization on the statistical properties of normalized data. Common strategies are evaluated, including normalization by fixed point (a single internal reference protein) and normalization by sum (aggregate measurement of data points, such as total protein staining). The effects of different strategies on the coefficient of variation (CV) and subsequent hypothesis testing are discussed.
