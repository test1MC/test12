Title: Normalization Methods for Quantitative Analysis of Western Blots

Speaker: Steve Shiflett, MS, Product Manager

Register here: licor.com/RegisterNormWebinar

Get practical, expert advice on how to normalize. Learn how to use different normalization methods to generate high-quality data suitable for quantitative comparisons.

  1. Western blot (WB) normalization corrects for unavoidable variability between samples on the blot
    1. More consistent, more accurate quantitative analysis and results
    2. Important to understand how WB normalization works and its impact on data analysis and coefficient of variation (CV)
  2. Standards for quantitative WB analysis have changed
    1. NIH concerns about variability, rigor, and reproducibility (1 - 3)
      1. Know how methods work, where variability and error arise
      2. Provide enough detail for readers, reviewers to see how data were collected and analyzed
    2. New reporting standards for quantitative WB analysis (4, 5) ask authors to clearly explain:
      1. How quantitative WB data were obtained
      2. Whether signal intensity has a linear relationship with antigen loading
      3. How protein loading was normalized among lanes

        4. -- The Journal of Biological Chemistry, Instructions for Authors (5)

    3. Be prepared for more rigorous publication standards regarding WB normalization
      1. Know the attributes of an accurate internal loading control
      2. Understand sources of variability in WB normalization
      3. Impact of normalization methods on data analysis and CV
  3. Normalization basics
    1. WB normalization mathematically corrects for unavoidable sample-to-sample and lane-to-lane variability by comparing the target to an internal loading control (6)
    2. Internal loading control (ILC) is an endogenous indicator of sample concentration and/or cell number
      1. Present in all samples and not affected by experimental conditions
      2. Enables relative comparison of target protein levels across the blot
    3. Ensures that changes in band intensity reflect actual biological change in samples
    4. Confirms that changes are not caused by variability in sample prep, gel loading, or transfer
  4. Commonly used internal loading controls
    1. Internal reference protein (7)
      1. Single, indirect indicator of sample concentration
      2. Housekeeping protein (HKP) is frequently used
      3. Unrelated endogenous protein that is stably expressed in all samples
    2. Total protein staining (6, 8-10)
      1. Uses all sample proteins in each lane as direct readout of sample concentration
      2. Membrane staining generally performed after transfer
      3. Covalent labeling methods sometimes used (Bio-Rad Stain-Free™, Amersham™ WB System) (11, 12)
    3. Post-translational modifications (PTM)
      1. Use target protein as its own internal loading control
      2. Pan/phospho analysis commonly performed (4, 5, 13)
      3. Combine modification-specific Ab with pan-Ab that recognizes target protein regardless of its modification state
      4. Analyze phosphorylation, glycosylation, ubiquitination, acetylation, and other PTMs (14)
  5. Plan ahead for accurate WB normalization
    1. Consider normalization strategy before beginning the experiment
    2. Choose method that is appropriate for experimental context and data analysis
    3. Manage WB variability for increased accuracy
      1. Experimental design (7, 10)
        1. Limit inherent variability of samples and methods
        2. Minimize amount of correction needed
        3. Detect changes more reliably, with smaller error bars and lower CVs
      2. WB normalization (6)
        1. Use appropriate ILC that is accurate indicator of sample concentration
        2. Correct for unavoidable variation
    4. No single normalization method or ILC will always be “best”
      1. ILC must be appropriate for:
        1. Context and biology of experiment
        2. Sources of variability in that specific experimental system
      2. Choice of ILC determines how accurately you can correct for variability
    5. The Core Principle of Normalization
      1. Target and ILC signals must vary to the same degree with sample concentration (6)
        1. Proportional relationship
        2. Consistent target-to-control ratio in all experimental samples
      2. Attributes of accurate, effective ILC (6)
        1. Signal intensity is proportional to sample concentration
        2. Signal intensity is proportional to abundance of the control
        3. ILC corrects for variation at all stages of WB process
        4. ILC is compatible with immunodetection of target proteins
  6. Using a housekeeping protein for WB normalization
    1. Actin, tubulin, or GAPDH typically used
    2. Is signal intensity proportional to sample concentration?
      1. ILC expression must be constant across all samples
      2. ILC expression must be unaffected by experimental conditions
      3. Vulnerable to biological variability in expression of ILC, because a single indicator is used (6)
      4. HKP changes the hypothesis (6)
        1. Original question: what is level of target protein, relative to sample concentration?
        2. New question: what is level of target protein, relative to concentration of HKP?
    3. HKP expression may be affected by experimental conditions and biological factors

      1. New recommendations from Journal of Biological Chemistry (4, 5)

        “‘House-keeping’ proteins should not be used for normalization without evidence that experimental manipulations do not affect their expression.”

        - The Journal of Biological Chemistry, Instructions for Authors (5)

      2. Example: α-tubulin and GAPDH expression affected by increasing cell density in culture (15)
      3. Example: Actin and tubulin are not stably expressed during rat retinal development (16)
      4. HKP may increase mean CV of normalized data (17)
        1. Sensitive to biological variability
        2. Higher CV may cause false negatives (small but genuine changes in level of target protein that are overlooked) (17)
    4. Is signal intensity proportional to abundance of control? (6)
      1. HKP and other abundant proteins generally produce strong bands
      2. Signal saturation occurs when a strong band exceeds capacity of the detector to record it
      3. High-intensity signals are not proportional to abundance
      4. Intensity of strong bands is underestimated, particularly with film
    5. Strong HKP bands often affected by signal saturation (6, 7, 10-12)
      1. Saturated bands = lost, irretrievable data
      2. Data loss alters quantitative analysis
    6. Summary: HKP can be used for WB normalization if appropriately validated
      1. Confirm that HKP is stably expressed in relevant samples and experimental conditions (4, 5)
      2. Confirm that both HKP and target are detected within linear range of assay, with no saturation (6, 7, 10, 11)
  7. Using total protein staining for WB normalization
    1. Total protein staining after membrane transfer (6, 8-10)

      1. New recommendations: Journal of Biological Chemistry (4, 5)

        “Normalization of signal intensity to total protein loading (assessed by staining membranes using Coomassie blue, Ponceau S or other protein stains) is preferred.”

        - The Journal of Biological Chemistry, Instructions for Authors (5)

      2. Total sample protein used as direct indicator of sample concentration (6)
      3. Monitors protein transfer across entire blot, at all molecular weights
      4. Workflow for REVERT™ Total Protein Stain and near-infrared (NIR) fluorescence imaging
        1. Use NIR fluorescent membrane stain to detect and quantify total sample protein (700 nm)
        2. Perform NIR fluorescent WB detection after total protein staining (800 nm)
    2. Is signal intensity proportional to sample concentration?
      1. Impact of biological variability greatly reduced, because many reference points are used
      2. Reduces mean CV of normalized data (17)
    3. Is signal intensity proportional to abundance of control?
      1. Validate linear range of detection for protein stain and sample loading
      2. Re-validation not needed for other antibodies or experimental conditions
      3. Total protein staining has wider linear range than HKP detection
    4. Summary: Total protein staining of the membrane is a useful method for WB normalization
      1. Direct, relevant measure of sample proteins on the blot
      2. Reduced impact of biological variability
      3. Emerging as new “gold standard” for WB normalization
    5. Covalent pre-labeling of total sample protein
      1. Bio-Rad Stain-Free™ technology (11, 12)
        1. Tryptophan residues in sample proteins labeled with fluorescent tag by UV crosslinking
        2. If epitope contains Trp, Stain-Free™ modification will interfere with antibody recognition (18)
        3. Labeling interference alters quantitative analysis, particularly for monoclonal antibodies
        4. May cause significant loss of signal (>75%) (18)
      2. Amersham™ WB System
        1. Sample proteins labeled with Cy5 reactive dye prior to electrophoresis
        2. Primary amines are labeled (N terminus and lysine residues)
      3. Summary: Covalent labeling is a source of variability
        1. Labeling is amino acid-dependent and may be inconsistent
        2. May affect immunodetection of target proteins
  8. Post-translational modifications (PTM) and pan/phospho analysis

    1. New recommendations: Journal of Biological Chemistry (4, 5)

      “Signals obtained using antibodies specific for phosphorylated epitopes should be normalized to the total protein level of the target protein.”

      - The Journal of Biological Chemistry, Instructions for Authors (5)

    2. Analysis combines phospho-Ab and pan-Ab (13)
    3. Monitor relative changes in level of modified protein across the blot (14)
    4. Avoids variability introduced by stripping and re-probing of blots (7)
  9. Summary: Several valid methods for WB normalization
    1. Understand how each method works
    2. Choose method that fits context and biology of experiment
    3. Control for WB variability accurately and effectively
    4. Ensure that data are rigorous enough to meet publication and funding needs in the future
